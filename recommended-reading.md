# Recommended Reading

This book carries no footnotes and no bibliography. That is a deliberate choice. To anchor every claim to a prior written authority would be to re-enact the very mechanism the book describes — the substitution of observed reality with the recorded word, the replacement of direct argument with citation chains that most readers cannot follow and few will check. If the argument here is sound, it should stand on its own. If it is not, no amount of footnoting will rescue it.

That said, some books and papers genuinely extend what is explored here, or arrived at related territory by different routes. They are listed not as sources or authorities but as good company — works worth spending time with for their own sake.

This list will grow as more of the book is published.

## On the evolutionary origins of human cooperation and language

**Sarah Blaffer Hrdy — *Mothers and Others: The Evolutionary Origins of Mutual Understanding* (2009)**
The argument that what made humans distinctively cooperative was not tool use or intelligence but the shift to shared infant care — alloparenting — which required reading the intentions of others at a depth no other primate needed. The foundation for understanding why the social brain developed the way it did. Directly relevant to the Prologue.

**Sarah Blaffer Hrdy — *Father Time: A Natural History of Men and Babies* (2024)**
Hrdy's more recent work, examining the conditions under which male investment in infants evolved, and what that tells us about the deep structure of human social bonds. A natural companion to *Mothers and Others*.

**Robin Dunbar — *Grooming, Gossip, and the Evolution of Language* (1996)**
The argument that language evolved primarily for social bonding rather than information transfer — that talking is, at its root, a form of grooming. Short, readable, and quietly radical. Changes how you hear a conversation.

## On oral culture and what writing changes

**Walter Ong — *Orality and Literacy* (1982)**
What actually changes when a culture moves from the spoken to the written word. Ong is meticulous about what oral cultures can and cannot do, and what literacy costs as well as gains. The Prologue's treatment of pre-written knowledge is in conversation with this throughout.

## On early symbolic behaviour and landscape knowledge

**Médard Thiry and Anthony Milnes — 'Palaeolithic Map Engraved for Staging Water Flows in a Paris Basin Shelter', *Oxford Journal of Archaeology*, Vol. 44, No. 1 (2025)**
The paper behind the Ségognole 3 rock shelter scene in Prologue II. The authors demonstrate that Palaeolithic people around 13,000 years ago engraved the shelter floor with a functioning model of local watercourses — the oldest known three-dimensional representation of a landscape. A short paper and freely available online.

---

## On the origins of writing and symbolic record-keeping

**Denise Schmandt-Besserat — *How Writing Came About* (1996)**
The accessible version of Schmandt-Besserat's landmark research into clay tokens — the small counters used across the ancient Near East for thousands of years before writing proper emerged. Her argument is that writing did not begin as a record of speech but as a record of goods: who had what, who owed what. The first symbols were not words but debts. Central to the argument in Chapter I.

**Ian Kuijt and Bill Finlayson — 'Evidence for Food Storage and Predomestication Granaries 11,000 Years Ago in the Jordan Valley', *PNAS*, Vol. 106, No. 27 (2009)**
The archaeological evidence behind the grain-pit scene in Prologue III. Excavations at Dhra' near the Dead Sea found purpose-built granaries with suspended floors — designed for air circulation and rodent protection — predating plant domestication by centuries. Four pages long and freely available online via PubMed Central.

## On ritual, sacrifice, and the first symbolic stores

**Jacobus van Dijk — 'Retainer Sacrifice in Egypt and in Nubia', in Jan N. Bremmer (ed.), *The Strange World of Human Sacrifice* (2007)**
The clearest scholarly treatment of the retainer burial evidence from Abydos and Ur that Chapter I draws on — the subsidiary graves of the First Dynasty kings, the scale of the killings, and what the archaeological record does and does not tell us about consent. Available as a PDF from the author's website.

**Laerke Recht — *Human Sacrifice: Archaeological Perspectives from around the World* (2019)**
A recent and rigorous survey of the archaeological evidence for human sacrifice across cultures and periods. Useful for situating the Ur and Abydos material in a broader pattern.
