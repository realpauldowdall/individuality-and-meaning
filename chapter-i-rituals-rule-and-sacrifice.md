# Chapter I: Rituals, Rule and Sacrifice

You sit alone with marks on a page. They move through your eyes into nerves, into the private loop. Nothing mystical happens, only chemistry and pattern recognition, but the result is permanent: what you knew five minutes ago is no longer what you know now. Even here, in this small act, you are shaped by the same force that shaped the first humans, the urge to hold something from one moment into the next, to find what matters through a world that changes. The marks before you are a pattern in words, made by the author, a fragment thought important enough to be stored and carried across time until it reaches you.

Two things can be observed. First, that you are an individual, second, that you were born into a society. For humans this has always been so. No infant survives without the care of others, and the long-immature human body made cooperation a survival necessity long before there was storytelling. In every environment, from coasts to savannahs to ice-edges, the conditions themselves pressed people together. To share watch over the young, to hunt large game, to defend against predators, to endure weather that could kill in a night. Out of this pressure came bands, kin groups, and, much later, villages, each an experiment in keeping children alive long enough to reproduce. From these experiments grew repeated acts that bound the group together, forming the first shared stores of meaning and action, guarded because they carried the pattern of survival.

In other animals, ritual acts such as courtship dances in birds, territory marking in wolves, rank displays in gorillas, grief in elephants, are embedded in the nervous system and triggered by the immediate cues of changing seasons, a familiar scent, the loss of an infant. Elephants have been seen to drag dead calves into hollows and cover them with soil, but beyond this our evidence for burial-like acts in animals is thin. Humans are different. Across the last hundred thousand years, in deserts, forests, tundra, and grasslands, the dead are treated in patterned ways. The body is set apart, placed, adorned, and covered. The act is repeated and passed on, part of the group’s protected store of behaviour.

There are stores of knowledge and stores of behaviour, and the environments that shaped them can still be read in the record. Yet they stand on an older layer, one pressed into being by the land itself. Where the hunt was uncertain, where plants ripened all at once and then were gone, where winter or dry season stood like a wall ahead, survival meant carrying the moment of plenty forward. Long before there were graves or stories, there were caches of what could be eaten later. The first store was not a shrine or a library, but food and water, hidden, guarded, or carried, that stood between the living and the empty season. This physical store, the cached surplus, remains the fundamental basis that enables humans and other animals to thrive.

Bound-limb burials start to appear in the European record about 5400 BCE: Brno-Bohunice (Czech Republic), Herxheim (Germany), and ten other Linearbandkeramik and post-LBK sites show adults or adolescents trussed hand-to-foot and covered while still alive. The practice persists into the Late Neolithic, last attested at Saint-Paul-Trois-Châteaux, France (≈3500 BCE). Parallel retainer graves mark the rise of early states. At Başur Höyük in upper Mesopotamia (3100-2800 BCE) ten teenagers were laid beside bronze spearheads and carnelian beads; at Abydos, Egypt (First Dynasty, 3100-2900 BCE) servants ring King Aha’s tomb; Kerma tumuli on the mid-Nile (≈1700 BCE) line bound retainers around the central shaft. Across all fifteen European and several Near-Eastern sites the pattern is consistent, humans are sacrificed to seal the elite’s status in death, kept and displayed as part of the their stored and guarded wealth.

Non-human rituals, wolf scent-marking, crane dances, elephant calf burials, follow fixed triggers, but these Neolithic live interments show learning and replication. They spread with shared ceramic styles, not with species-wide instincts. Language and repeated instruction let communities treat living people as items of exchange. This was part of a larger symbolic economy, a system that depends on the ability to hold something, whether food, goods, or lives apart from immediate use and release it only by permission. By 5400 BCE, then, the story of measurement and substitution is already under way. To see its roots we must look even earlier, to the first objects counted and the first debts recorded.

Standardised counting begins in Mesopotamia around 9000-8000 BCE, when herders and storekeepers press small clay, stone, or bone shapes into service as stand-ins for goods. A cone equals one peck of barley (≈5.3 kg), a sphere, a one-litre oil jar, a cylinder, a healthy sheep, a four-sided pyramid, a day’s labour. Other shapes track finished items, biconical for tools, ovoid for beer or honey, disk for flour or dairy. A pouch of such tokens functions as a portable ledger: whoever holds the pieces holds the claim, and whoever guards them controls when the goods they represent can be taken or withheld.

In Mesopotamia starting around 3700 BCE, clerks seal mixed token sets inside clay envelopes (bullae) and roll each shape across the wet surface before firing. They then add a silver weight, one shekel, roughly 8.3 g, so the packet doubles as both inventory and price tag: 34 barley cones, say, match a single shekel. The same silver weight can also settle for one sheep, ninety days of basic labour, or the volume of fourteen modern grain sacks of barley. Because these sealed packets had to be kept safe until opened, they became a controlled store, and access to their contents depended on permission. These ratios circulate widely in the region and anchor contract law, tax quotas, and wages.

After 3300 BCE the envelope itself flattens into a tablet. A stylus presses wedge strokes that replace the three-dimensional tokens by marks on a two dimensional wet clay shape. These are the same marks, cylinders for sheep, hour-glass symbol for tools but now they are signs and counts. The clerk no longer needs the objects. The marks alone fix quantity, commodity, and value. The marks, like the goods once tallied, could now be stored, guarded, and released only to those authorised to use them. From these account tablets the wedge script, cuneiform, expands. Centuries later it carries poetry such as the Epic of Gilgamesh, but its first task is still clear on every line. Record who owes what, and in what measure

Across four separate regions, southern Mesopotamia, the Nile Valley, the Maya lowlands, and late-Shang China, writing appears between about 3300 BCE and 1200 BCE. The earliest tablets, tags, and bone inscriptions are tallies. Uruk IV (Mesopotamia) clay records list barley rations issued by temple granaries, ivory labels in an Abydos (Nile Valley) tomb log oil jars delivered to a king, Shang oracle (China) bones note animals owed for sacrifice, early Maya glyph blocks track tribute to a regional ruler. Each record solves three practical limits of speech alone: (1) monitoring growing food surpluses, (2) coordinating labour drawn from wider territories, and (3) stamping elite authority onto ritual calendars. Temple accountants, palace stewards, and court diviners fixed number, commodity, and name in clay, wood, bone, or stone so the claim could outlast a conversation and travel beyond the speaker. These records could be held apart from immediate settlement and released only under the authority of those that guarded it. These marks let obligations be reassigned at a distance and disputed after the fact. In its first centuries, writing served as portable memory for surplus accounting and power negotiation, not as literature.

Royal courts used writing to freeze memory. Early-Dynastic Sumerian tablets log reigns and festival days, Abydos ivory tags brand a pharaoh on each annual rite, Shang oracle bones place every offering on a numbered day, Classic Maya stelae pin accessions to Tzolk’in and Haab. Once a name and date sat in clay, stone, or bone a rival could not dispute it. The same habit sorted labour. Girsu (Sumer, Mesopotamia) corvée lists assign canal gangs, Old-Kingdom (Egyptian) quarry tags count stone-cut crews, a Shang (Henan, China) scapula tallies men for a border fort, and a Maya (Bonampak, Mexico) mural lists maize-field workers. Writing therefore served two tasks at once: anchoring sovereign memory and scheduling far-flung work through records kept under elite custody, where access to the instruction or the schedule itself required permission.

After tokens and sealings showed that numbers could outlive speech, temple stewards and palace scribes used the same trick for social ties. An Ur III debt tablet names a tenant, the barley due, and the deadline, Old-Kingdom quarry tags list a crew and its stone quota, a Shang bone records which diviner approved which scapula and the pig owed if the omen rings true. Writing turned a spoken promise into a fixed assignment: a steward defines, a scribe copies, and a judge checks months later, with the written record itself stored and protected as the arbiter of what would be released or enforced. By the mid-third millennium BCE, the pattern first glimpsed in the bound-limb graves of Brno (Czech Republic) and Herxheim (Rhineland, Germany) had hardened into royal doctrine. In the Royal Cemetery of Ur (Iraq, Mesopotamia), cylinder-seal impressions in sixteen tombs name Queen Pu-abi and list the musicians, grooms, and cup-bearers buried beside her. At Abydos (Upper Egypt), the tomb of King Djer bears his Horus name alongside roughly 318 subsidiary graves, each retainer identified by a small limestone stela pressed into the wall beside the body. Anyang (Henan) pit M1004 records the clan signs of fifty-three captives killed for Queen Fu-Hao. A Late-Preclassic Maya panel at Chiapa de Corzo (Chiapas, Mexico) shows four bound prisoners captioned with Calendar-Round dates. Each inscription links a death to royal authority and to a festival or accession. The mark does more than describe, it authorises. By equating a person with a line of script, officials could shift lives across ledgers as easily as grain or silver. Sacrifice, once episodic, became recordable, schedulable, repeatable. Once entered, it could be stored, guarded, and acted on only when those holding the record gave permission. Abstraction made the order to kill portable and harder to resist.

Ur ration tablets, Anyang oracle bones, and Maya tribute murals each show that a written order can outlive its speaker. Names, quotas, and due dates fixed on clay, bone, or plaster allow others to confirm or enforce the claim long after the conversation ends. A steward reading the tablet is entitled to collect barley, a commander reading the oracle bone may claim prisoners. Marks that began as grain tallies therefore became references for social rank and obligation, kept as formal stores of authority that could be unlocked at will by the holder.

Ur death-tally tablets, Shang scapulae that note ‘three men, five cattle’ for an eclipse rite, and Maya lintels carved with bound captives follow the same format: payer, quantity, settlement day. The debt-tally at Ur lists bread for labourers, the Shang record counts both people and cattle for sacrifice, the Maya lintel names captured nobles taken in war. Each inscription sets ruler or temple, victim, and occasion, treating sacrifice as a liability to clear. The record itself remains stored as evidence, protected from alteration, and brought out only to confirm the debt was met. When the ritual payment is made, the tablet, bone, or stone stands as proof that the ledger is closed.

A linen docket from Old-Kingdom Egypt lists how much bread a mason receives, a swipe card today stamps an employee ‘late’. Both records sort people into categories that trigger fixed responses. When a credit-score update shifts a loan rate, or a missed payment brings a rent notice, the same rule applies. In every case, grain tallies in Ur, scapulae in Anyang, lintels in Chiapa de Corzo, marks on clay, bone, or stone outlast the speaker and decide a fate, their force coming from being stored under recognised control until the moment they are used. Today, a family loses their home because a cell in a spreadsheet turned red, in the past, a retainer was sent to the grave on the strength of a name pressed into clay.

The next chapter turns from these marks and numbers to the landscape itself, oak survey poles, cedar tally sticks, palm-leaf grants, to show how living trees once became boundary marks and debt notes, each one a stored and guarded claim on the land, binding whole forests to the ledger.
